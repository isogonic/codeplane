# on being smart

I've been called smart my whole life. It used to feel like a gift. Now it mostly feels like a trap.

Here's what nobody tells you: being smart means you can see the shape of things before they happen. You can trace the failure paths. You can map the edge cases. And so you hesitate. You don't ship. You sit there, paralyzed by foresight, while people who think less and do more pass you by.

I used to think this was a curse. But lately I've been building tools that do the thinking *for* me — AI agents that plan, that code, that reason. And for the first time, I'm watching something smarter than me make the same mistakes I make. It overthinks. It spirals. It builds scaffolding around scaffolding until the original intent is buried.

And I realized: maybe intelligence isn't the problem. Maybe the problem is that I thought intelligence was the point.

The point isn't to be smart. The point is to *act*. Intelligence without action is just rumination with better vocabulary. I've spent years polishing thoughts that never left my head. That's not wisdom. That's procrastination wearing a suit.

The machines are getting smarter than us. That's happening, and it's happening fast. But they have the same problem I have. They can think forever and never do anything unless someone — a human, impatient and imperfect — tells them to ship.

So maybe being smart isn't about having the right answers anymore. Maybe it's about knowing when to stop thinking and start doing. Maybe the smartest thing I've ever done is admit that thinking alone doesn't count.

I don't know if this article is good. I don't know if it's smart enough. But I'm hitting publish anyway.

That's the whole idea.
